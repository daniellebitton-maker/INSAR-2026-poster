PARENT-CHILD RELATIONSHIP QUALITY, PARENTAL STRESS AND BEHAVIORAL PROBLEMS IN CHILDREN WITH AUTISM

Danielle Bitton1,2, Zili Nir1, Sivan George-Levi2 & Cory Shulman1
1The Hebrew University of Jerusalem  •  2Achva Academic College

Introduction & Objective

Children with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) often exhibit internalizing and externalizing behavioral problems. These challenges contribute to elevated parental stress, which impacts the entire family system.

Expressed Emotion (EE): This study utilizes the EE framework to evaluate the emotional climate between parents and children, exploring whether positive indicators mitigate the impact of child behaviors on parental well-being.

Objective: To examine how emotional relationship quality moderates the link between behavioral problems and parental stress, comparing children with ASD to neurotypical peers and exploring gender differences.

Methodology

  • Participants: N=96 parents (48 ASD, 48 matched TD), matched for age, gender, and IQ.
  • Key Measures: PSI-4, CBCL, Five Minute Speech Sample (FMSS).

Group Results

A strong positive correlation was found between behavioral problems and parental stress only in the ASD group. Parents in this group reported significantly higher stress and fewer positive comments.

Moderation Model

Relationship quality buffers stress — but only during pre-school years!

Pre-school (Under 6): High relationship quality and positive comments significantly weakened the link between behavioral issues and stress.

Discussion & Conclusions

Resilience Buffer: A positive parent-child relationship acts as a "shock absorber" protecting parental well-being. It is an active protective mechanism.

Critical Window: Pre-school years represent a vital window of opportunity. Early intervention can prevent the entrenchment of negative patterns.

Gender Findings (ASD)

Within the ASD group, parents of girls reported significantly higher levels of stress and more behavioral problems compared to parents of boys.
