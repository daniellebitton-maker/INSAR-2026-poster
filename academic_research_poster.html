PARENT-CHILD RELATIONSHIP QUALITY, PARENTAL STRESS AND BEHAVIORAL PROBLEMS IN CHILDREN WITH AUTISM

Danielle Bitton1,2, Zili Nir1, Sivan George-Levi2 & Cory Shulman1
1The Hebrew University of Jerusalem  •  2Achva Academic College

Introduction & Objective

Children with ASD often exhibit behavioral problems. These challenges contribute to elevated parental stress, which impacts the entire family system. This study uses the Expressed Emotion (EE) framework to evaluate how emotional relationship quality moderates this link.

Objective: To examine if positive relationship indicators buffer the impact of behavioral problems on stress, and whether this varies by diagnosis, gender, and child age.

Methodology

  • Participants: 96 parents (48 ASD, 48 matched TD), matched for child age, gender, and IQ.
  • Key Measures: Parenting Stress Index (PSI-4), Child Behavior Checklist (CBCL), Five Minute Speech Sample (FMSS).

Group Results

Behavior and stress were highly correlated in the ASD group (r=.58, p < .001), but no significant correlation was found in the TD group. Parents of children with ASD reported significantly more Emotional Over-Involvement (EOI) and fewer positive comments.

Gender Findings (ASD)

Within the ASD group, parents of girls reported significantly higher levels of stress and more behavioral problems compared to parents of boys (p=.05).

Moderation Model

Relationship quality buffers stress — but only during pre-school years!

Pre-school (Under 7): High relationship quality and positive comments significantly weakened the link between behavioral issues and stress.

Discussion & Conclusions

Resilience Buffer: Positive parent-child relationships may act as a "shock absorber", particularly during the early diagnostic window where high relationship quality significantly buffers stress (p < .05).

Critical Window: Parents of younger children (pre-school) reported significantly higher stress and expressed more criticism compared to parents of school-aged children (p < .001).

Gender Vulnerability: Girls with ASD exhibited more behavioral symptoms, highlighting the need for gender-sensitive clinical screening and early support.
